# Malaysia HR Interview Preparation — English Speaking Round

> Context: Buổi phỏng vấn với HR bên Malaysia, 100% bằng tiếng Anh. Họ quan tâm nhiều đến English communication, confidence, clarity, energy, soft skills, motivation, salary, availability và overall fit.

## 0. Mindset quan trọng

Buổi này không chỉ kiểm tra nội dung trả lời, mà kiểm tra cách bạn giao tiếp:

- Speak clearly.
- Speak confidently.
- Keep good energy.
- Smile and maintain eye contact with camera.
- Answer directly, then add example.
- Do not speak too fast.
- Do not sound like reading from a script.
- Do not use AI or other devices during the interview.
- Join meeting 5–10 minutes early.
- Turn on camera.
- Prepare quiet environment, stable internet, good lighting.

## Q13. How do you make sure communication is clear in remote meetings?

### Answer

> I usually prepare before the meeting, write down key points, and speak clearly. If something is unclear, I ask follow-up questions. After important discussions, I like to summarize the decision or action items, either verbally or in a message, to make sure everyone has the same understanding.

## Q14. What will you do if you don’t understand a requirement in English?

### Answer

> I will not pretend that I understand. I will ask clarification questions, repeat my understanding, and confirm with examples. For example, I may say: “Let me confirm my understanding. Do you mean that the user should be able to approve the transaction before it is submitted?” This helps avoid misunderstanding and rework.

## 8. Soft skills questions

## Q15. How do you handle conflict with a teammate?

### Answer

> I try to focus on the problem, not the person. If there is a disagreement, I will listen first, then explain my point with reasons and trade-offs. For technical decisions, I prefer comparing options based on maintainability, delivery time, risk, and business impact.
>
> If we still cannot agree, I will involve the team lead or discuss with the team to make a decision together. The goal is not to prove that I’m right, but to choose the best solution for the project.

## Q16. How do you handle pressure or tight deadlines?

### Answer

> When there is pressure, I try to stay calm and prioritize. I identify the most critical tasks, clarify what must be delivered first, and communicate risks early. If the deadline is tight, I discuss with the team whether we can reduce scope, use a phased delivery, or prepare a rollback plan.
>
> I don’t like taking shortcuts that can create serious production or security risks, especially in banking or enterprise systems.

## Q17. How do you work with QA/BA/Product teams?

### Answer

> I work closely with QA, BA, and Product to clarify requirements and expected behavior. If a requirement is unclear, I ask questions and confirm with examples. With QA, I discuss test scenarios, edge cases, and defects. With BA/Product, I try to understand the business reason behind the requirement, not only the technical task.

## Q18. How do you handle feedback?

### Answer

> I’m open to feedback because it helps me improve. If the feedback is technical, I try to understand the reason and apply it to my work. If I disagree, I will discuss respectfully and provide my reasoning. But overall, I see feedback as part of teamwork and continuous improvement.

## Q19. How do you mentor junior developers?

### Answer

> When mentoring junior developers, I try to explain the reason behind my suggestions, not just tell them what to change. For example, in code review, I explain why transaction boundary, error handling, or test coverage matters. I also like to share examples, pair review code, and help them gradually take more ownership.

## Q20. What are your strengths?

### Answer

> My strengths are backend development, ownership, and communication. Technically, I have strong experience in Java, Spring Boot, microservices, messaging, and cloud-based systems. In terms of working style, I’m responsible, proactive, and careful about code quality and reliability.
>
> I also work well with cross-functional and international teams. I try to communicate clearly, raise issues early, and support the team to deliver good results.

## Q21. What are your weaknesses?

### Answer

> One weakness I had earlier was that I sometimes tried to make a solution too complete from the beginning. Over time, I learned to work more iteratively: clarify requirements early, deliver the most important part first, and improve the solution step by step.
>
> This helps me balance quality and delivery better, especially in Agile projects.

## Q22. Tell me about a time you had a production issue.

### Answer

> In production issues, my first priority is to reduce user impact. I check logs, metrics, recent deployments, and identify which component is failing. Then I work with the team to decide whether to rollback, hotfix, or apply a temporary mitigation.
>
> After the issue is resolved, I believe it is important to do a follow-up: identify root cause, improve monitoring, add tests if needed, and document lessons learned to prevent the same issue from happening again.

## 12. Confidence and speaking practice

## Speaking rules

- Start with a clear sentence.
- Use short paragraphs.
- Pause after important points.
- Do not rush.
- Smile slightly.
- Keep your voice energetic.

## Useful phrases

### To clarify

> Could you please clarify that question?

> Let me confirm my understanding.

> Do you mean the technical side or the project/business side?

### To buy time naturally

> That’s a good question. Let me think for a moment.

> Sure, I can explain that.

### To structure answer

> I would explain it in three parts.

> First..., second..., and finally...

### To show confidence

> Yes, I’m comfortable with that.

> Yes, I have experience in that area.

> I’m confident I can adapt quickly.

### If you do not know something

> I haven’t worked deeply on that specific area, but I understand the concept and I’m willing to learn quickly.

> In my previous project, I worked on a similar problem, so I believe I can pick it up.
